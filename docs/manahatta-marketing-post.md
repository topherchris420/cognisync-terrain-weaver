# Why I named a climate platform after an island of hills

*Launch post for Manahatta, the Vers3Dynamics urban resilience platform. Short
social variants at the end.*

---

Everything I build starts in the same place: outside.

I've loved nature for as long as I can remember — the way a forest floor
swallows a downpour without a puddle, the way a creek finds its line through
a hillside, the way land, left to itself, already knows how to handle water.
That love is the foundation of this project, and it's why the platform
carries the name it does.

Before Manhattan was a grid, it was **Manahatta** — the island of many hills.
Four hundred years ago it was hills and streams, chestnut forests and salt
marshes, one of the most ecologically alive places on the continent. The
[Welikia Project](https://www.welikia.org/) reconstructed that 1609 landscape
block by block, and it's my favorite version of New York City: proof that
under every city there is still land, and the land remembers what it's for.

That memory matters more now than ever. When rain hits a meadow, the ground
drinks it. When rain hits pavement, it has nowhere to go — so it goes into
basements, subways, and living rooms. Most cities can't tell you, street by
street, how much of their surface can still breathe. I wanted anyone to be
able to ask that question and get a real answer.

**Manahatta** is that answer. Point it at any satellite view of any city on
Earth, and in seconds it returns:

- **A land-cover breakdown** — vegetation, water, soil, buildings, pavement —
  read from the imagery by AI
- **An Urban Absorption Score** — a single 0–100 number, weighted by real
  runoff coefficients, that says how well that patch of city absorbs a storm
- **A flood-risk band** — low, moderate, or critical
- **Four adaptation strategies** — prioritized green, blue, and gray
  infrastructure recommendations you can hand to a planner, a council, or a
  neighborhood group
- **A shareable, exportable report** — every analysis becomes a link and a
  PDF, and joins a public feed of scans from around the world

It's open source (MIT), it runs on free satellite imagery, and there's no
paywall between you and the question "how resilient is my block?" Because the
point was never to sell nature back to people. The point is to make the
ground under our cities visible again — to give the hills, streams, and
wetlands buried under the pavement a voice in how we adapt to the climate
we've made.

Manahatta was a place where land and water worked together. It can be again —
everywhere. Run a scan of your neighborhood, share what you find, and if you
write code, come build with us.
